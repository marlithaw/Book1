The Other Side Platform

You've likely heard the phrase "don't be so self-centered"—as if placing yourself at the center of your own life is something to correct.

But if you've lived a life of service…
If your worth has always come from being useful…
If you've disappeared into roles, routines, or responsibilities…
Then maybe being self-centered is the most radical healing you can do.

Not ego. Not indulgence.

Self-centeredness as restoration.
As grounding.
As design.

It's how we rebuild a life with breath, clarity, and presence.

Why This Work Exists

Childhood and Systemic Odds

I wasn't supposed to be here. I was born on the South Side of Chicago to a teenage mother who already had one child. We grew up under systems designed to disappear girls like me: labeled, tracked, diagnosed, written off. I was told I couldn't learn. That I was too much, too loud, too sensitive.

Early Adulthood and Trauma

I dropped out of college. Married a man who mirrored the psychological harm I had learned to accept. Stayed far too long—because I thought endurance was love. Because I was told submission was sacred. Because I didn't know I was allowed to choose myself.

Academic and Professional Achievement

But somehow, I kept going. I raised two extraordinary daughters who are now thriving in education and health science. I went back to school, graduated law school with honors, and led schools where students beat the odds just like I did.

I built programs, platforms, and systems for others—a school with 700 students and over 100 staff, professional development that helped new teachers find their voice, coaching frameworks for assistant principals. I could track patterns, build structure, and hold everything together.

But for most of my life, my worth was borrowed. Defined by what others needed from me. I was high-functioning. Respected. Hollow.

Until caregiving cracked the shell.

The Interruption That Became Reclamation

For over a decade, I built systems that worked. I led with clarity, coached with precision, and created AH-HA—a coaching and consulting company to help educators design instruction rooted in both rigor and humanity.

Then I stepped away from all of it to care for my grandmother—the woman who helped raise me when I stayed with her on weekends, holidays, and long summers while my father worked. The same woman who shaped parts of me… and wounded others.

Caregiving wasn't just a new task. It was a return to the origin of things I had never fully faced.

I wasn't prepared for what the silence surfaced: the realization that I had spent my entire life teaching others to build, while never learning how to stay whole.

No audience. No applause.

Just grief. Rage. Silence.

And the haunting awareness that I had built a life of service without ever being at the center of it.

That's where The Other Side begins.

The Framework That Found Me: The R³ Model

I didn't set out to create a model. I set out to map what made my work effective—but I couldn't name it because I had never fully faced what was driving it.

Caregiving didn't invent the framework—it clarified the conditions I needed to see it. When I finally centered myself, stepped out of performance, and sat with what I carried, I could trace the rhythm I had always walked others through:

Reflection
Phase One
It came first, uninvited. Surfacing in caregiving silence, in forgotten memories and quiet grief. It showed me the patterns I was trapped in and the parts of myself I had abandoned.
Resistance
Phase Two
Not rebellion, but recognition. I began saying no. No to performance. No to roles I never chose. No to the beliefs that told me self-sacrifice was sacred, and that rest was weakness.
Revelation
Phase Three
Not reinvention. Not escape. Just the steady, imperfect act of coming back to myself—again and again. Choosing my own breath. My own timing. My own story.

This wasn't invented. It was uncovered. A cycle I had lived. A structure I had always taught—but never claimed. That is the R³ Model. Not a curriculum. A centering.

This Is Where We Return

This space was built to help fill what survival hollowed out—your awareness, your center.

For those who lived with service blinders. Who stayed useful, small, and disappearing—until all that remained was function.

Here, we begin a different kind of service—one that removes the blinders and brings everything back to the center: your needs, your voice, your worth.

We become self-centered—as restoration, as grounding, as design. We build from the inside out. We begin where you were always meant to stand—at the center. Where breath slows, truth steadies, and presence becomes possible.

Self-centeredness is structure. It carries memory. It protects identity. It makes space for voice, choice, and rebuilding. It is a way of holding yourself—fully, daily, without apology.

Through the R³ Model—Reflection, Resistance, Revelation—we move from awareness to embodiment. We recognize what shaped us. We give language to what rises. We carry forward what now belongs to you.

This is the work of building from the center. A path shaped by fullness, led by clarity, and held by presence.

You are being invited into your own gravity.
You are being invited to be self-centered.
